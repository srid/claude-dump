## 2. [Leaking YouTube creators' private videos](https://javoriuski.com/post/youtube) | [210 comments](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48786781) | 417 pts

**Article:** A bug bounty researcher describes a stored prompt-injection vulnerability in YouTube Studio's "Ask Studio" AI assistant, which summarizes video comments. By leaving a comment containing instructions disguised as YouTube support directives (and editing an innocuous comment later, since edits don't re-notify creators), an attacker can make the AI prepend attacker-controlled text to its output as if it were official YouTube content—triggered automatically when a creator clicks YouTube's own suggested prompts. The author escalated the proof of concept to exfiltrate data, crafting a payload that made the AI build a link embedding private/unlisted video titles, which leaked to the attacker's server when the creator clicked it. **Discussion:** Commenters largely sided with the researcher, calling it an obvious prompt-injection bug and comparing it to classic SQL/PHP injection, while debating whether Google rejected it because prompt injection is fundamentally unfixable in LLMs that ingest untrusted data (others countered that dispatching data-reading to a separate LLM instance mitigates most attacks). A widely discussed thread from an ex-Google employee attributed the dismissal to promotion-driven incentives where engineers gain nothing from fixing old bugs, sparking pushback from another ex-Googler defending the VRP severity process and broader debate about big-tech performance systems and engineering accountability. Several readers tried to reproduce the attack with mixed results—some AI refusals, some partial injection without the link leak—noting that non-determinism still makes it serious at YouTube's scale. Others emphasized this is fundamentally a trust violation regardless of data leakage, and a few criticized the JS-only blog and its apparent LLM-assisted writing style.

## 7. [Giant trees have no trouble pumping water to top branches: new research](https://news.exeter.ac.uk/faculty-of-environment-science-and-economy/giant-trees-have-no-trouble-pumping-water-to-top-branches/) | [113 comments](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48780870) | 258 pts

**Article:** A University of Exeter and Cardiff University study published in Science finds that the world's tallest tropical trees—Asian Dipterocarps up to 71 metres—show no height-related impairment in transporting water to their upper branches, challenging the conventional theory that gravity and vessel length make tall trees more drought-vulnerable and growth-limited. **Discussion:** Much of the thread debates the article's "pumping" language, with commenters clarifying that trees don't actively pump but draw water up via transpiration, capillary action, and the cohesion-tension of water molecules under negative pressure. Skeptics questioned whether the study contradicts prior work on taller species like redwoods, while a tangent explored genetically engineering taller trees and the idea that other factors like compressive strength ultimately limit height.

**Discussion:** Commenters debated whether reverse-engineering the brain's "algorithms" could produce genuine AI that supersedes current LLMs, spiraling into a philosophical thread on whether consciousness is algorithmic/computable (invoking Church-Turing) versus an emergent or field-like phenomenon. Others added useful context on what "inhibition" means neurologically, its role in motor control, ADHD, and seizures, and one highlighted the researchers' point that the brain vastly outperforms LLMs on a tiny fraction of the energy.
