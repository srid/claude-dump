## 3. [For first time, a cell built from scratch grows and divides](https://www.quantamagazine.org/for-the-first-time-a-cell-built-from-scratch-grows-and-divides-20260701/) | [208 comments](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48747304) | 600 pts

**Article:** Synthetic biologist Kate Adamala's team assembled a cell-like structure (nicknamed "spudcells") from non-living molecular components that, for the first time in a from-scratch system, can grow, replicate its DNA, and divide into daughter cells. The cells are built from liposomes containing a minimal synthetic genome, DNA-replication machinery, roughly 36 commercial enzymes, and supply liposomes carrying ribosomes, tRNA, and nutrients, with modified membrane proteins enabling division through molecular crowding. The system remains far from true life: it cannot make its own ribosomes or metabolic enzymes, requires constant external feeding, lacks waste-removal and defense systems, and does not evolve on its own. Adamala likened the achievement to a "Wright flyer" rather than a modern cell.

**Discussion:** Commenters debated the significance of "making a living thing out of dead components," with several noting the practical goal is likely custom manufacturing—engineering fully synthetic cells with bespoke metabolic pathways to produce fuels, drugs, or materials—while others argued editing existing organisms via CRISPR or precision fermentation is an easier shortcut. A recurring thread weighed biosafety and "grey/green goo" fears, with multiple people pointing out the cell's dependence on prebuilt ribosomes and external feeding makes runaway self-replication implausible. Others discussed how the near error-free DNA replication means the cells barely evolve (a plus for production consistency), and whether the structure counts as "alive" at all, drawing analogies to crystals and brain-dead patients. A long tangent argued over the relative carbon-emission contributions of cars, AI data centers, and electricity generation.

**Article:** Copybara is an open-source Google tool for transforming and moving code between repositories, most commonly to sync code between an internal monorepo and external public repositories. Rather than being a simple mirror, it applies configurable transformations during a sync—such as path/directory remapping, file exclusions, and stripping of internal references—defined in workflow config files. **Discussion:** Commenters, several of them current or former Googlers, describe Copybara's main use as syncing an internal monorepo outward to GitHub (and occasionally importing changes back), emphasizing that its real value is the transformations—path remapping, file filtering, header/bug-ID stripping, build-system translation between Bazel and internal Blaze—rather than plain mirroring. Multiple people note that one-way exports work well while bidirectional sync gets messy because transforms can't always be cleanly inverted once repos diverge, and that for pure mirroring the tool is overkill compared to git subtree or GitLab's mirroring. The thread compares it to alternatives like Rust's Josh, Meta's (now-abandoned) fbshipit, git-subtree/git-filter-repo, and Jujutsu-based workflows, with some reporting Copybara being slow on remote repos.
