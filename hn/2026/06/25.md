## 1. [An entire Herculaneum scroll has been read for the first time](https://scrollprize.org/firstscroll) | [182 comments](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48675179) | 803 pts

**Article:** The Vesuvius Challenge announced that PHerc. 1667 ("Scroll 4") became the first carbonized Herculaneum papyrus to be digitally unrolled and read in full, without physically opening it. Researchers used high-resolution X-ray scanning at the European Synchrotron Radiation Facility, then reconstructed and virtually flattened the scroll's geometry and applied machine learning to detect faint ink on the charred surface, with the Vesuvius Challenge community, the National Library of Naples, and EduceLab collaborating. The recovered text is identified as a philosophical treatise on ethics, with evidence pointing to a Stoic work that names Aristocreon (nephew of Chrysippus) and dates to the 2nd century BC. Two additional scrolls also yielded results: higher-resolution imaging confirmed earlier readings of PHerc. Paris 4, and PHerc. 139 revealed its title as Philodemus's "On Gods, Book 8"; all data, code, and transcriptions were released openly.

**Discussion:** A member of the Vesuvius Challenge team (verditelabs) hosted an extended AMA-style thread, explaining that none of the Herculaneum ink is visible by spectrum alone, so unwrapping, segmentation, and ML/physically-based rendering are needed, and that PHerc. 1667 was unusually small with readable ink. Commenters probed the risk of ML "hallucinating" ink, with the team noting errors are local (e.g., slightly altered strokes) and unlikely to fabricate idiomatic Greek or Latin, and emphasizing that the data and models are openly available. Much discussion centered on what the scrolls might contain — hopes for lost histories and unknown works versus a papyrologist's expectation of mostly Epicurean philosophy, though commenters noted only one of three scrolls so far looks Epicurean. Other threads touched on the philosophical/religious history of Epicureans and Stoics, lost ancient literary works, and praise for self-taught contributors who joined the team after winning competition prizes.
